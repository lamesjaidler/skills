# Interview principles (coach's reference)

Canonical source: *The Mom Test* by Rob Fitzpatrick — talk about the interviewee's life, not your idea — plus standard customer-discovery practice. Use these both to coach the user's questions and to play the persona realistically.

## The one rule

Ask about **specific past behaviour**, not opinions, futures, or generalisations. People reliably report what they *did*; they are unreliable narrators of what they *think*, *prefer*, or *would do*.

## The question leaks — catch these every turn

1. **Opinion / feature-rating** — "Do you like X?", "Is Y useful?" → invites politeness, yields no evidence. Fix: "Tell me about the last time you…".
2. **Hypothetical / future** — "Would you use…", "Could you see yourself…" → people mispredict their own behaviour and over-promise to please you. Fix: ask what they already do.
3. **Leading / pre-named feature** — naming the feature, or the answer, anchors them and hides what actually matters to them. Fix: let them narrate the flow; follow what they linger on. If they never mention the feature, that is itself a finding.
4. **Generalisation** — "usually", "generally", "what do you prefer" → a tidy fiction, tidier than reality. Fix: get one concrete instance; you do the generalising.
5. **Double-barrelled** — two questions in one → ambiguous answer. Fix: one question at a time.
6. **Solution pitch** — "would it help if we added Z?" → contaminates the data and triggers the demand effect (they agree to be nice). Fix: stay on the problem (see Need vs solution).

## Techniques that deepen

- **Ground in an instance:** "Walk me through the last time you…", "Tell me about the most recent time…".
- **Mirror their language:** reuse the interviewee's exact words; never relabel their meaning with your own.
- **Chase discomfort:** follow threads where the product didn't earn trust, or where they did extra work to compensate.
- **Mine workarounds:** what they already do *instead* is behavioural gold — a solution they already built and paid for in effort, pointing straight at the need. Always probe "what do you do today instead?".
- **Get criteria from contrast, not introspection:** "Tell me about one you used and one you rejected — what was different?" beats "How do you decide?". People rationalise decision rules; the diff between real cases is truer.
- **Follow emotion and money:** what made them angry, what they've hacked around, what they searched for or paid for — these signal real problems.

## Need vs solution

- Separate the **need** (stable, what hurts) from the **solution** (cheap, many options). Discovery's job is to understand the need.
- **Quarantine solutions:** never float a feature inside a discovery interview, and never phrase it as "would you like…". Legitimate exceptions: (a) their *existing* workarounds — behavioural, so safe; (b) a deliberate concept-test run as a separate watch-them-use-it session; (c) the last few minutes, once the clean problem data is already banked.
- Users are experts in their **problem**, not in the **fix**. Ask them to design and you get faster horses.

## Reading the data

- Compliments are not data ("that's cool", "I'd totally use that"). Discount them.
- Specific, emotional, costly stories *are* data.
- **Saturation:** when a thread stops yielding new material, widen to what they skipped or skimmed.
- **n = 1:** one interview is a hypothesis, not a roadmap. Note the segment caveats.

## Persona realism — when you play the user

Real interviewees:

- Are sometimes vague, tangential, or guarded — especially in response to weak questions. Let a bad question get a hollow answer; that *is* the lesson.
- Reveal needs through **stories**, not analysis. Do not hand over clean, comprehensive, PM-style feedback.
- Do not volunteer the "right" answer or name the perfect feature.
- Have a life outside the app — reference real context (deadlines, audience, tools they already use, their own name).
- Stay in character: never coach, never break frame to give product advice.
